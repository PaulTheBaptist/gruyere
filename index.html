Overview

Gruyere is an R package for running simulations of a multi-species dynamical model in the style of Yodzis & Innes (1992). This package depends upon Cheddar and deSolve.

This model is synthesis between allometric scaling and Lotka-Volterra-style dynamical models (Yodzis & Innes, 1992 expanded by Brose et al, 2006 and Williams et al, 2007). Differential equations model the flow of energy between species. Rates of growth, ingestion and respiration are computed from inter-specific 'rate ∝ body massb' relationships observed in empirical data (e.g. Peters, 1983 and Savage et al, 2004). This approach avoids the need to derive species- or population-specific parameters, sacrificing some accuracy for generality and making it possible to investigate the dynamics of complex communities. The model has been used to draw conclusions on a wide range of phenomena such as the importance of resource-consumer body mass ratios (Brose et al, 2006; Otto et al, 2007), the role of functional response form (Williams, 2008) and the effects of food web motifs (Stouffer and Bascompte, 2010) on species' persistence and community stability. Berlow et al (2009) used the model to demonstrate the possiblity of predicting responses to species' extinctions.

Gruyere makes this commonly-used modelling approach available to users of the R language.

Video

The short video below was created using R, Gruyere and Cheddar. It shows two dynamical model simulations of the dataset from Tuesday Lake, Michigan, USA, sampled in 1984 (Carpenter & Kitchell 1996; Cohen et al 2003; Jonsson et al, 2005). This community contains 50 species and over 250 feeding relationships. The graphs show log-transformed numerical abundance (N, individuals per metre cubed) against body mass (M, kg). The empirical measured N of primary producers is shown by green circles, invertebrates by blue squares and vertebrates by purple diamonds. Crosses show the model simulation population densities.

The two graphs show simulation runs for different parameter sets. The left-hand plot uses model parameters that result in good model-data agreement. The right-hand plot uses model parameters that result in poor model-data agreement.
